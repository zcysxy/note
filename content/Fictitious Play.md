# Fictitious Play

In real life, players may be myopic and only [[Best Response|best respond]] to other players without calculating or playing a [[Nash Equilibrium]] strategy. However, when playing a game repeatedly, they may adapt their strategy based on the history of other players' actions and eventually converge to a Nash equilibrium. This process is ==learning in games==, as players adapt their _beliefs_ about other players and act rationally w.r.t the beliefs.

Fictitious play is an iterative method of calculating [[Nash Equilibrium]] using the above idea without actually playing the game repeatedly.
Formally, each player maintain a belief that uniformly mixes the other players' historical actions $\mu _{i} = \frac{1}{t}\sum_{\tau=1}^{t} s_{-i}^{\tau}$, and [[Best Response|best respond]] $\sigma_{t+1}\in \operatorname{BR}_{i}(\mu _{i})$.
Note that in fictitious play, players have a _common belief_ as it's generated by the observed history of actions.

> [!thm] Convergence of strategies
>
> Let $(\sigma ^{t})_{t\in\N}$ be the strategy profile sequence generated by fictitious play. If there exists $T$ such that $\sigma ^{t}=\delta _s$ with $s\in S$ for all $t\ge T$, then $s$ is a [[Nash Equilibrium|PSNE]].
> And if there exists $T$ such that $\sigma ^{T}=s^*$ with $s^*$ being a strict [[Nash Equilibrium|NE]], then $\sigma ^{t} = s^*$ for all $t\ge T$.

> [!def] Convergence of beliefs
>
> We say $(\sigma ^{t})_{t\in\N}$ converges to a strategy profile $\sigma\in \Sigma$ in the time-average sense if $\mu _{-i} ^{t}(s_{i}) \to \sigma(s_{i})$ as $t\to \infty$ for all $i$ and $s_{i}$, i.e.,
>
> $$
> \lim_{ t \to \infty } \frac{1}{t}\sum_{\tau =1}^{t} \mathbb{1}\{ s_{i}^{\tau} = s_{i} \} = \sigma(s_{i}),
> $$
>
> where $\sigma (s_{i})$ is the marginal probability of $\sigma$ on $s_{i}$.
