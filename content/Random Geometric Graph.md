# Random Geometric Graph

- Motivation: We want to capture spatial or geometric constraints in real-world networks over a given space
- Notation: $G(n,r)$
- Model: Fixed number of $n$ nodes uniformly sampled over a given metric space, e.g., $[0,1]^{2}$ with Euclidean distance, undirected, form an edge between two nodes if their distance is less than or equal to $r$
  - To avoid dealing with boundary effects, we can also use the toroidal distance: $\dist(x,y) = \min_{z\in\Z^{2}}\|x_{1}-x_{2}+z\|$
- [[Network Phenomena#Phase Transition|Phase transitions]] in terms of $r(n)$
  - Connectivity: $t(n) = \sqrt{ \ln n /n }$
    - There exists $c_{1}>c_{2}>0$ such that the graph is connected if $r(n) \ge c_{1} \sqrt{ \ln n /n }$ and disconnected if $r(n) \le c_{2} \sqrt{ \ln n /n }$
  - [[Network Phenomena#Giant Component|Giant component]]: $r \approx \sqrt{\frac{1}{\pi n}}$ for $[0,1]^{2}$ with Euclidean distance

## Poisson Point Process

A Poisson point process generalizes the [[Poisson Process]] to higher dimensions but enjoys similar properties.
Specifically, a counting process $N: X\to \N$ on space $X$ is a (homogenous) Poisson point process if

1. for any disjoint sets $A_{1},\dots,A_{k}$, the r.v.s $N(A_{1}),\dots,N(A_{k})$ are independent, and
2. for any bounded and closed set $A$, $N(A) \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda|A|)$ for some $\lambda >0$ and $|A|$ is the volume of $A$.

Some properties of a [[Poisson Process]] also enjoyed by a Poisson point process:

- Conditioned on $N(A)=n$, the $n$ points in $A$ are i.i.d. uniformly distributed over $A$.

Analyzing a random geometric graph using a Poisson point process with parameter $\lambda=n$ is backed by the following theorem:

> [!them] Tauberian
>
> For any function that acts on $G(n,r)$ and is non-decreasing in $n$, let $f(N)$ be the corresponding random variable when $f$ is evaluated on nodes generated by a Poisson point process with intensity $\lambda=n$. Then, if $\mathbb{E}[f(N)]\asymp \Theta (n^{\alpha})$ for some $\alpha>0$, then $f(n) \asymp \Theta(n^{\alpha})$.

## Connectivity

We evenly partition $[0,1]^{2}$ into $m^{2}$ squares.
Then, a sufficient condition of connectivity is that each square has at least one node, and that of disconnectivity is that 8 squares around a center square are empty.
For any square to have no node, the probability is $\exp(-n /m^{2})$.
Let $5 /m^{2} = r(n)^{2}-\epsilon$ for some $\epsilon>0$.
Then, the probability of the complement of the first event is bounded above by

$$
m^{2} \exp(-n /m^{2})
\le \frac{5}{r^{2} - \epsilon} \exp(-n(r^{2}-\epsilon) /5).
$$

Let $r = \sqrt{ 5a \ln n /n }$ for some $a \ge 1$. We get

$$
m^{2}\exp(-n /m^{2}) \lesssim \frac{n^{1-a}}{a \ln n }  \to 0.
$$

Thus, $c_{1} = \sqrt{5.1}$ works for connectivity.

For disconnectivity, let $m=r(n)^{-1}$. Then, the probability of a square that has a node but all its 8 neighbors are empty is

$$
p_{0} \coloneqq (1-\exp(-n /m^{2})) \exp(-8n /m^{2}).
$$

Let $r = \sqrt{ b \ln n /n }$ for some $b\in(0,1 /8)$. We get

$$
p_{0} = (1 - n^{-b})(n^{-8b}) \gtrsim n^{-8b}.
$$

Then, the number of such squares is lower bounded by

$$
\frac{m^{2}}{9}p_{0} \gtrsim \frac{n^{1-8b}}{9b \ln n} \to \infty.
$$

Since the number of such squares follows a [[Binomial Distribution]], its variance is smaller than its mean. Then, by the [[Moment Method]], we have the probability of having at least one such square, and thus the probability of disconnectivity, approaches 1. Thus, $c_{2} = \sqrt{0.1}$ works for disconnectivity.
